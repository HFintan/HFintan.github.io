Research Interests
Compressed Sensing:
As part of the Theoretical and Computational Optimization Research Group in the University of Birmingham, I am collaborating to develop a generic algorithmic framework and convergence theory for seeking the sparsest solution of a general model. This work has applications in signal recovery and denoising, image reconstruction, statistical estimation, and ﬁnancial engineering amongst others. More speciﬁcally, I am working on the construction and manipulation of deterministic and structured compressed sensing matrices and algorithms for efﬁcient recovery of compressed data.

Computational Homology:
My doctoral thesis focused on developing some theory and software for efficiently computing the homology of Euclidean data sets in up to four dimensions. This software was an extension of work done by Graham Ellis as part of Hap, a homological algebra library for use with the GAP computer algebra system. This extension exploited certain mathematical advantages of using permutahedrally-based structures, rather than traditional simplicial or cubical ones, to speed up and expand the scope of certain implementations of homological image computations.
The software should be available here soon, along with the relevant manual, tutorial and libraries.

To see how the manifold nature of permutahedral complexes can be advantageously applied to knot computations in the real world, see the paper Computing Fundamental Groups from Point Clouds by Brendel, Dłotko, Ellis, Juda, and Mrozek, which investigates the application to the analysis of protein complexes.
